Module 1

The Blueprint — Designing Your Relationship

Move from "we're just winging it" to "we have an agreement."

🎧 Listen to Module 1

🎧 Audio Lesson: Rules vs. Values

Most couples who try to open their relationship start with rules. Pages of rules. "You can't sleep with anyone who lives in our city. You can't sleep with someone more than twice. You can't stay the night." And on and on.

Here's the problem: rules are built on fear. They're designed to control outcomes. And the moment life throws you something unexpected — which it will — the rules break. And then both of you feel betrayed, even if no one technically did anything wrong.

We tried the rules-based approach. It didn't work. What works? Values.

The Value System

Instead of 30 rigid rules, have 3-5 core values that guide your behavior. For example:

  • Transparency: We tell each other what's happening — before, during, or after.
  • Safety: We always use protection. No exceptions.
  • Priority: Our relationship comes first. If something is hurting us, we pause.

These values are flexible. They evolve. They don't break the first time someone catches feelings or changes their mind about a rule.

✎ Exercise: The "Hard Limits" Audit

This is your first exercise as a couple. Here's how it works:

  1. Sit down separately. Don't talk to each other yet.
  2. Write your 3 absolute hard limits. These are the things that are non-negotiable. Examples: No exes. No mutual friends. Safe sex mandatory. No sleepovers.
  3. Come back together. Share your lists.
  4. Find the overlap. Whatever you both agree on — those are your only rigid rules. Everything else is negotiable.

The point: You don't need 50 rules. You need 3-5 that you both genuinely agree on, grounded in your shared values.

Module 2

The Pitch — How to Open Up

Have "The Conversation" without a fight or a breakup.

🎧 Listen to Module 2

🎧 Audio Lesson: The Scary Conversation

This is the part most people dread. How do you tell your partner you want to open the relationship? How do you bring it up without them thinking you're unhappy, that they're not enough, or that you've already cheated?

Here's the truth: there's no perfect way to say it. But there's a wrong way — and we've seen it happen a lot. Don't bring it up during a fight. Don't blurt it out after drinks. Don't casually mention it and then say, "Just kidding, unless you're into it." All of those damage trust before the conversation even starts.

How Ali Brought It Up

I brought this up to my partner about a year into our relationship. I didn't come with an ultimatum. I came with curiosity. I said: "Hey, I've been thinking about something, and I want to share it with you because I value our honesty."

That one sentence changed everything. She didn't make it about what she wanted to do. She made it about what she valued — honesty. And that opened the door.

The 3-Step Script

If you're the one bringing it up, follow this structure:

✎ The 3-Step Script

Use these exact steps when you sit down with your partner:

Step 1 — Validate

"I love our life. I'm incredibly attracted to you. Nothing about this conversation means I'm unhappy."

Step 2 — Express (Use "I" Statements)

"I feel like I have a side of me that wants to explore [X]. Not because you aren't enough — but because I want to experience [Y] and I want us to do it together."

Step 3 — The Safety Net

"I only want to do this if it brings us closer. If at any point it hurts us, we stop. You are my priority."

That's it. Three steps. Validate. Express. Protect.

After you say your piece, shut up. Seriously. Let them process. Don't fill the silence. Don't over-explain. Let them respond.

💬 What If They Say No?

They might. And that's okay. If your partner says no, it's not a rejection of you — it's a boundary. Respect it. Don't push. Don't sulk. Say: "Thank you for being honest. I respect that completely. I'd rather have this conversation and know where we stand than never bring it up."

Sometimes "no" becomes "maybe" six months later. Sometimes it doesn't. Either way, you've planted a seed of honesty, and that matters.

📅 Homework

Schedule a "State of the Union" date night. No phones, no TV. Bring the 3-Step Script, either written or memorized. This is the most important conversation you'll have in this course.

Module 3

The Safety Net — Jealousy & Aftercare

Creating a protocol for when feelings get hurt.

🎧 Listen to Module 3

🎧 Audio Lesson: The Jealousy First-Aid Kit

Jealousy. Let's talk about it. Because if you open your relationship, it will show up. Maybe on the first night. Maybe three months in. But it will come. And if you don't have a plan for it, it will destroy everything.

Most people treat jealousy like a red flag. "If I feel jealous, it means we shouldn't be doing this." Wrong. Jealousy is a check engine light. It's telling you something needs attention. It's not telling you to stop driving.

The 3 Types of Jealousy

  • Fear of Replacement: "They're going to leave me for this person."
  • Fear of Missing Out: "They're having more fun than me."
  • Ego Jealousy: "I don't like the idea of someone else touching my partner."

Each one has a different root. And each one has a different solution. The first step is always the same: name it. Don't say "I'm jealous." Say "I feel like I'm being replaced" or "I feel left out." That gives your partner something to work with.

✎ The "Gremlin" Protocol

This is your emergency protocol for when jealousy hits in real time. It's simple, and it works.

Step 1: The Code Word

Pick a code word or phrase that means "I'm feeling jealous and I need reassurance." It can be anything — "Monkey," "Red light," "SOS." Something that doesn't sound dramatic but signals that you need a check-in.

Step 2: Partner's Response

When you hear the code word:

  • Pause what you're doing.
  • Reassure. Not defensively — genuinely.
  • Don't say: "You're being ridiculous." Do say: "I hear you. I'm right here. What do you need?"

Step 3: Your Job

When you say the code word:

  • Identify the specific fear. Not "I'm jealous" — but "I feel like I'm being replaced" or "I feel left out of something."
  • Ask for what you need: "Can you text me when you're on your way home?" or "I need 10 minutes of just us before we talk about it."

✎ The Reconnection Ritual (Aftercare)

After one of you has a date or experience with someone else, what happens next? This is where most couples mess up. They either ignore it or over-talk it.

Create a Reconnection Ritual — a set of actions you always do after a date with someone else. Examples:

  • 10 minutes of cuddling, no talking.
  • A shower together.
  • "Don't tell me details until morning."
  • A specific meal you cook together.

The ritual isn't about controlling the narrative. It's about coming back to each other. It says: "No matter what happened out there, this is home."

Module 4

The Hunt — Apps & Finding Partners

How to actually find people without being creepy or getting scammed.

🎧 Listen to Module 4

🎧 Audio Lesson: The Meat Market

You've had the conversation. You've set your values. You've got a jealousy protocol. Now the next question is: where do you actually find people?

The Apps

  • Feeld: The gold standard for non-monogamous couples. It's designed for this. You can link profiles, set preferences, and filter by what you're looking for.
  • Tinder: Still works, but you have to be clear in your bio. If you don't mention you're in a relationship, you're starting with a lie.
  • 3Fun, #open: Niche apps that work well in bigger cities but can be dead in smaller ones.
  • Local Apps: Wherever you're traveling, check what's popular locally. In some countries, there are apps you've never heard of that are way more active than Tinder.

Couples Profile vs. Separate Profiles

This is a big one. Many couples create a single profile — "John & Jane." Don't do that. Joint profiles tend to feel impersonal and sometimes predatory. Instead, create two separate profiles, and mention each other in the bio. Link your profiles on Feeld. On Tinder, add a photo together. This way, the third person gets to talk to a real individual, not a committee.

✎ The Profile Audit

Your profile is your first impression. Make it count.

Photos

  • Photo 1: Clear face shot. Smiling. Good lighting.
  • Photo 2: Full body shot. No sunglasses. Show your vibe.
  • Photo 3: Lifestyle shot — traveling, cooking, doing something interesting. Shows personality.

Bio

State exactly what you're looking for. Be specific. The more specific, the better the matches. Examples:

  • "ENM couple, looking for a fun third for travel adventures. We're clean, communicative, and drama-free."
  • "Ethically non-monogamous. In a relationship with [name]. She knows and approves. Looking for casual connections."

Don't be vague. Vague profiles attract vague people.

✎ The 3-Question Vetting Screen

Before meeting anyone, ask these three questions:

  1. "Have you played with a couple/person in a relationship before?" — Experience matters. Someone who's new to this might catch feelings or not respect boundaries.
  2. "What are your boundaries?" — If they can't answer this clearly, that's a red flag.
  3. "When was your last test?" — Non-negotiable. If they dodge this, unmatch.

Three questions. That's it. If they pass all three, you can move to a casual meet (coffee, drink — always in public first).

📅 Homework

Set up your profiles (separate ones) on at least one app. Use the Profile Audit as your checklist. Have your partner review your profile before going live.

Module 5

The Lifestyle — Travel & Logistics

Executing this while on the road — the Ali special.

🎧 Listen to Module 5

🎧 Audio Lesson: Solo vs. Duo Play

This module is the one that's specific to us — and to anyone who travels as a couple while living the lifestyle. Most ENM advice assumes you're in one city, with one routine. But we've done this across 30+ countries — while living in Canada, Mexico, and Thailand — and the logistics change everything.

Solo Play (Hall Passes)

This is when one partner goes out solo — a date, a hookup, a night out — while the other stays home or does their own thing. It's the most common form for travel couples.

Ground rules for solo play:

  • Always share your live location.
  • Set a check-in time (e.g., "Text me by 11 PM").
  • Have a safe word that means "come get me" — no questions asked.
  • Debrief the next day using the Reconnection Ritual from Module 3.

Duo Play (Together)

This is when both of you engage together — threesomes, clubs, parties. It's a completely different dynamic and requires a different set of rules.

Ground rules for duo play:

  • Eye contact check-ins: look at your partner every 15-20 minutes. A smile means "I'm good." A raised eyebrow means "check on me."
  • Tap-out signal: a hand on the shoulder means "I want to leave — now."
  • Never split up at a venue unless you've both explicitly agreed.
  • Debrief together after. This is non-negotiable.

✎ The "Location Scout" Technique

You're in a new city. How do you find the lifestyle scene?

Step 1: Research Before You Land

Google "[city name] swinger clubs" or "[city name] lifestyle clubs." Check Feeld activity in the area. Look at Reddit threads for recommendations.

Step 2: Safety Protocol

  • Share live location with each other at all times.
  • Set check-in times.
  • Have a "get-out" plan — know your exit route, have cash for a taxi, keep your phone charged.
  • Never leave your drink unattended.

📄 Download: Travel Safety Checklist

Your go-bag checklist for lifestyle travel:

  • Condoms (your own — don't rely on anyone else's)
  • Cash (always have local currency)
  • Burner phone or secondary SIM info
  • Local emergency numbers
  • Uber/taxi app installed and ready
  • Hotel address written down (in case your phone dies)
  • Location sharing turned on with your partner
